FIXED INCOME AND RATES: THE VOL DRIVER MOST TRADERS MISS

Why Equity Traders Must Understand Rates

Most equity and options traders treat the bond market as someone else's problem. This is a serious mistake. Interest rates are the gravitational force that affects every asset class. When rates move, equity valuations change, volatility regimes shift, and cross-asset correlations rearrange themselves.

The bond market is approximately $50 trillion in the US alone — more than double the equity market. It is dominated by institutional players (banks, pension funds, insurance companies, central banks) who move slowly but with enormous size. When the bond market sends a signal, equity traders ignore it at their peril.

Understanding rates does not mean becoming a fixed income trader. It means understanding the signals that rates and credit markets send about economic conditions, liquidity, and risk appetite — signals that often lead equity market moves by days or weeks.

The MOVE Index: Bond Market VIX

The Merrill Lynch Option Volatility Estimate (MOVE) Index is to the Treasury market what the VIX is to the equity market. It measures the implied volatility of Treasury options across the 2-year, 5-year, 10-year, and 30-year maturities.

Typical MOVE levels:
- 60-80: Calm. Rates are stable, monetary policy is predictable. This range prevailed during much of 2019 and mid-2021.
- 80-100: Normal. Moderate uncertainty about the Fed's path or economic outlook.
- 100-130: Elevated. Active policy debate, inflation surprises, or fiscal uncertainty.
- 130-160: High stress. Major policy shifts, banking concerns, or economic regime changes.
- Above 160: Crisis. The MOVE has exceeded 160 only a handful of times — the 2008 financial crisis (264), the March 2020 COVID crash (164), and the March 2023 SVB banking crisis (199).

MOVE vs VIX correlation: This is where the signal gets powerful. MOVE often leads VIX by days or even weeks. The logic: credit and rates markets are driven by institutional flows and are more "informed" than the equity market (which has heavier retail participation). When Treasury volatility spikes, it signals that institutional investors are uncertain about the economic outlook — and that uncertainty eventually reaches equity markets.

Empirical relationship: When MOVE rises above 120 and VIX is still below 20, equity traders should be on alert. This divergence (high rates vol, low equity vol) has preceded equity selloffs multiple times. In September 2023, MOVE rose to 140 while VIX remained around 17. Within two weeks, VIX jumped to 23 and the S&P 500 sold off 5%.

When MOVE and VIX diverge in the other direction (MOVE falling while VIX is elevated), it suggests the equity market is overpricing risk relative to the rates market. This divergence favors selling equity volatility.

The Yield Curve: The Economy's Crystal Ball

The yield curve plots interest rates across maturities, from 3-month Treasury bills to 30-year Treasury bonds. Its shape contains critical information about economic expectations.

The 2s10s spread (10-year yield minus 2-year yield) is the most-watched curve metric:

Normal (positive, 100-250bp): Long-term rates are higher than short-term rates, reflecting expectations of future growth and inflation. The economy is healthy. Banks earn money by borrowing short and lending long (the "carry trade"). This spread prevailed during most of 2009-2019.

Flat (0-50bp): Short-term and long-term rates are converging. The economy may be slowing. The Fed is potentially too tight. Flat curves prevailed in 2018-2019 as the Fed hiked into a slowing economy.

Inverted (negative): Short-term rates exceed long-term rates. This is the market's clearest recession signal. An inverted 2s10s spread has preceded 8 of the last 8 recessions since 1960. The lead time varies from 6 to 24 months.

The 2s10s inverted in July 2022 and reached -108bp by July 2023 — the deepest inversion since the early 1980s. The curve remained inverted for over two years, the longest sustained inversion in modern history.

Steepening dynamics: After an inversion, the curve steepening phase is often when the recession actually begins. The steepening happens because the Fed starts cutting short-term rates (2-year yield falls) while long-term rates may rise (inflation concerns or supply worries). The 2s10s steepening out of inversion is often called a "bull steepener" if both rates fall (but short rates fall faster) or a "bear steepener" if both rates rise (but long rates rise faster). The bear steepener variant (seen in late 2023) is more concerning for equities because it means long-term borrowing costs are rising even as the economy weakens.

Fed Funds Futures: Reading the Fed's Playbook

The Fed does not surprise markets as often as headlines suggest. Fed funds futures and the CME FedWatch Tool show the market's probability-weighted expectation for the fed funds rate at each upcoming FOMC meeting.

How to read it: If the current fed funds rate is 5.25-5.50% and the March fed funds futures contract implies a rate of 5.10%, the market is pricing in approximately a 60% probability of a 25bp cut by March.

Key concepts:

Number of cuts/hikes priced: Add up the implied rate changes across all upcoming meetings to see the full expected path. In early 2024, the market was pricing in six 25bp cuts for the year (150bp total). By April 2024, after hot inflation data, this had been repriced to only one or two cuts. That repricing drove significant equity and bond volatility.

Surprise = volatility catalyst: The Fed's actual decision matters less than the delta between the decision and what was priced in. If the market expects no change and the Fed cuts, that is a dovish surprise (bullish for equities, bearish for the dollar, lower rates vol). If the market expects a cut and the Fed holds, that is a hawkish surprise (bearish for equities, bullish for the dollar, higher rates vol).

The dot plot: Four times a year, FOMC members project their expected rate path (the "dots"). The median dot is the market's anchor for forward rate expectations. When the median dot shifts hawkishly (higher) or dovishly (lower) relative to market pricing, it triggers repricing across all asset classes.

For options traders: FOMC meetings are scheduled vol events, similar to earnings. IV rises into the meeting and collapses after. The VIX typically drops 1-3 points on FOMC days when the outcome is in line with expectations. But a true surprise — like the Fed hiking when cuts were expected — can cause VIX to spike 5-10 points.

TIPS Breakevens: The Market's Inflation Thermometer

Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) are government bonds whose principal adjusts with CPI. The difference between a nominal Treasury yield and a TIPS yield of the same maturity is the "breakeven inflation rate" — the market's expectation for average inflation over that period.

Key measures:

5-year breakeven: Currently-priced inflation expectation for the next 5 years. Typically 2.0-2.5% in a stable environment. Above 3% signals inflation concerns. Below 1.5% signals deflation fears.

5-year, 5-year forward: The market's inflation expectation for the 5-year period starting 5 years from now. This is the Fed's preferred measure of long-term inflation expectations because it strips out near-term noise. It typically anchors around 2.0-2.3%. When it rises above 2.5%, the Fed becomes more hawkish (fighting inflation expectations). When it falls below 2.0%, the Fed becomes more dovish (worried about deflation).

During 2021-2022, 5-year breakevens rose from 2.0% to 3.6% — the highest level since the TIPS market was created. This screamed "inflation is not transitory" months before the Fed acknowledged it. Equity markets eventually sold off as the Fed was forced into aggressive rate hikes.

For equity traders, rising breakevens are a headwind for growth stocks (higher expected inflation → higher discount rates → lower present value of future earnings) and a tailwind for energy, materials, and other inflation beneficiaries.

Credit Default Swaps: Insurance on Default

Credit default swaps (CDS) are insurance contracts against a borrower defaulting on their debt. The annual premium (spread) reflects the market's assessment of default risk.

Key benchmarks:

Investment Grade CDX (IG CDX): A basket of 125 investment-grade corporate issuers. Normal spread: 50-70 basis points (0.50-0.70% annual premium). Elevated: 80-100bp. Stress: 100-150bp. Crisis: above 150bp (reached 280bp in March 2020 and 615bp in 2008).

High Yield CDX (HY CDX): A basket of 100 high-yield (junk bond) issuers. Normal spread: 300-400bp. Elevated: 400-500bp. Stress: 500-700bp. Crisis: above 700bp (reached 1,100bp in March 2020 and 1,800bp in 2008).

Credit spreads as equity signals: Credit and equity markets both reflect corporate health, but credit often moves first. Credit analysts and bond traders tend to identify deterioration before equity analysts and stock traders because they focus on cash flow and balance sheet quality.

When IG CDX widens from 60bp to 100bp while the S&P 500 is flat or only slightly lower, it is a warning. Credit is pricing in risk that equities have not yet acknowledged. This divergence preceded the equity selloff in late 2018 (credit widened in October, stocks crashed in December) and in early 2020 (credit widened in late February, stocks crashed in March).

When credit spreads tighten while equities are weak, it is bullish. Credit is saying the fundamentals are healthier than equity prices suggest. This happened in October 2022 — HY spreads peaked in July and were tightening even as the S&P 500 made new lows in October.

MBS Spreads and Convexity Hedging

Mortgage-backed securities (MBS) are the largest fixed income market after Treasuries. The spread of MBS yields over Treasury yields carries information about financial conditions and, importantly, creates a unique source of interest rate volatility.

The convexity problem: MBS have "negative convexity." When rates fall, homeowners refinance their mortgages, shortening the expected life of MBS. When rates rise, refinancing stops, extending the expected life. This means MBS investors are always getting the wrong duration at the wrong time.

To manage this, MBS investors (primarily banks and the Fed) engage in "convexity hedging" — buying Treasuries (or receiving in swaps) when rates fall, and selling Treasuries (or paying in swaps) when rates rise. This hedging activity amplifies rate moves: it is procyclical, creating a feedback loop where rate movements cause hedging that causes further rate movements.

The impact on equity vol: In periods of high MBS convexity hedging (typically when rates are near levels where significant mortgage pools would refinance), Treasury volatility increases. This elevated rates vol spills into equity vol through the MOVE → VIX relationship.

In 2023, as the Fed reduced its MBS holdings and banks faced unrealized losses on their MBS portfolios (a factor in the SVB collapse), MBS spread dynamics became a significant driver of Treasury and equity volatility.

The Reverse Repo Facility: The Liquidity Gauge

The Federal Reserve's Overnight Reverse Repurchase Facility (ON RRP) is where money market funds and other institutions park excess cash overnight, receiving Treasuries as collateral. The total balance reflects the amount of excess liquidity in the financial system.

Reading the gauge:

High RRP balance (above $1.5 trillion): Abundant excess liquidity. Money market funds have so much cash that they lend it to the Fed. This was the case throughout 2022-2023, when the RRP peaked at $2.55 trillion in December 2022. Abundant liquidity generally supports asset prices and suppresses volatility.

Declining RRP balance: Excess liquidity is being absorbed. As the Fed's quantitative tightening (QT) removes reserves and as Treasury issuance absorbs cash from money markets, the RRP declines. In 2023-2024, the RRP declined from $2.5T to below $500B. This liquidity drainage creates a less supportive environment for risk assets.

Near-zero RRP: No excess liquidity. The financial system is operating with tight reserves. This is when bank reserves can become scarce, repo rate spikes can occur (as in September 2019), and financial plumbing issues can trigger volatility.

The pace of RRP decline matters more than the level. A rapid decline (like the $1.5T drop in H2 2023) can coincide with tightening financial conditions even if the absolute level is still positive.

Real Rates: The True Cost of Money

The real interest rate is the nominal rate minus expected inflation. The cleanest measure is the TIPS yield, which directly reflects the real rate investors demand.

10-year TIPS yield interpretation:

Deeply negative (below -1%): Extremely accommodative financial conditions. This prevailed in 2020-2021 (-1.0% to -1.2%) and was a massive tailwind for growth stocks, crypto, and speculative assets. When the real cost of money is negative, everything with future cash flows gets bid up.

Slightly negative to zero (-0.5% to 0%): Accommodative but normalizing. The transition zone.

Positive (0% to 2%): Restrictive. Capital has a real cost. Long-duration assets (growth stocks, unprofitable tech, REITs) face headwinds because future cash flows are discounted more heavily. In 2023, the 10-year TIPS yield reached 2.5% — the highest since 2007 — and growth stocks significantly underperformed value stocks.

Above 2%: Very restrictive. Historically, 10-year real rates above 2% have preceded equity corrections. The logic: at a 2%+ real rate, risk-free Treasuries offer genuine competition to risky assets.

The equity valuation connection: The S&P 500 P/E ratio has a strong inverse relationship with real rates. When real rates were -1%, the market P/E expanded to 23-24x. When real rates rose to 2%, the P/E compressed to 17-18x. Each 1% move in real rates corresponds to approximately 2-3 multiple points of P/E compression or expansion.

The Dollar (DXY): The Global Tightening Mechanism

The US Dollar Index (DXY) measures the dollar against a basket of six major currencies (EUR, JPY, GBP, CHF, SEK, CAD). The dollar is the world's reserve currency, and its strength or weakness functions as a global financial condition indicator.

DXY interpretation:

Below 95: Weak dollar. Generally supportive of US equities (multinational earnings worth more), emerging markets (dollar-denominated debt is easier to service), and commodities (priced in dollars, so cheaper for non-US buyers).

95-103: Neutral range.

Above 103: Strong dollar. Headwind for US multinational earnings (translation effect). Stress for emerging markets and dollar-denominated debtors. Suppresses commodity prices. In 2022, DXY hit 114.8 — the highest in 20 years — and it coincided with a brutal bear market in EM equities and bonds.

Dollar and equity vol: A rapidly strengthening dollar (DXY rising more than 1% per week) has historically preceded VIX spikes. The mechanism: a strong dollar tightens global financial conditions, stresses carry trades (especially yen and EM currencies), and forces deleveraging by global investors.

The dollar smile theory: The dollar strengthens in two opposite scenarios — US economic outperformance (capital flows into the US) and global crisis (flight to safety into dollar assets). It weakens in the middle (synchronized global growth, risk appetite high). This means a strong dollar can be either good news (US economy leading) or bad news (global panic). Context matters.

Case Study: March 2023 — SVB and the MOVE Spike

The Silicon Valley Bank collapse in March 2023 demonstrates how rates volatility can rapidly spill into equity and credit markets.

Background: Throughout 2022, the Fed raised rates from 0% to 4.5%. Banks holding long-duration bonds (including Treasuries and MBS) suffered massive unrealized losses. SVB, with a concentrated deposit base of tech startups, was especially vulnerable.

Timeline:
- March 8: SVB announces a $1.8 billion loss on bond sales and a $2.25 billion capital raise. The stock drops 60% after hours.
- March 9: MOVE Index jumps from 115 to 135. VIX barely moves (still at 21). Credit is leading equities.
- March 10: SVB fails. FDIC takes over. MOVE spikes to 167. VIX rises to 26. Bank stocks crater — KRE (regional bank ETF) drops 15% in two days.
- March 13: Signature Bank fails. Credit Suisse reports "material weakness." MOVE hits 199 — the highest since 2008. VIX reaches 26. IG CDX widens from 72bp to 92bp. HY CDX widens from 430bp to 530bp.
- March 15: Credit Suisse receives a $54 billion lifeline from the Swiss National Bank.
- March 19: UBS agrees to acquire Credit Suisse.

The signal: MOVE led VIX by 2-3 days at every step. Traders watching MOVE had advance warning. When MOVE spiked from 115 to 135 on March 9 (a 17% move), VIX was still at 21. An equity trader who bought VIX calls or SPY puts on the MOVE signal would have profited handsomely as VIX eventually caught up.

The rate dynamics were remarkable. The 2-year Treasury yield fell from 5.07% to 3.98% in three days — a 109bp move that normally takes months. Fed funds futures went from pricing additional hikes to pricing cuts within two weeks. This violent repricing in the rates market drove everything: bank stock losses, credit spread widening, equity vol, and eventually the Fed's decision to pause hiking.

The lesson: Rates vol (MOVE) is the canary in the coal mine. Credit spreads (CDX) are the early warning. Equity vol (VIX) is the lagging indicator. Watching all three — and especially their divergences — gives you a significant informational advantage.

Key Takeaways

1. The MOVE Index measures bond market volatility and often leads VIX by days or weeks. MOVE above 120 with VIX below 20 is a warning sign.
2. The 2s10s yield curve inversion has predicted 8 of 8 recessions since 1960. The steepening out of inversion is when the recession typically begins.
3. Fed funds futures show the market's rate expectations. Surprises relative to pricing — not the absolute decision — drive volatility.
4. TIPS breakevens are the market's inflation expectation. Rising breakevens favor value/commodities; falling favor growth/tech.
5. Credit spreads (CDX) often lead equity moves. Widening credit with flat equities is a bearish warning.
6. MBS convexity hedging amplifies rate moves and spills into equity vol through rates volatility.
7. The reverse repo facility measures excess liquidity. Rapid declines signal tightening conditions.
8. Real rates (TIPS yields) above 2% have historically preceded equity multiple compression and corrections.
9. A rapidly strengthening dollar (DXY) tightens global financial conditions and precedes VIX spikes.
10. Watch the sequence: MOVE first, credit second, VIX third. Divergences between these three indicators are high-value trading signals.
