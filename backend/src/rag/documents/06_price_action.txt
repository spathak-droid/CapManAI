PRICE ACTION: READING THE MARKET'S LANGUAGE

What Is Price Action?

Price action is the study of raw price movement on a chart — without relying on indicators like moving averages or RSI. It is the purest form of market analysis because it looks directly at what buyers and sellers are actually doing, not at a mathematical derivative of what they did.

Think of it this way: indicators are like reading a summary of a book. Price action is reading the book itself.

Professional traders often say that price is the only indicator that is never late. Every other indicator is calculated from price, which means it lags. Price itself leads.

Candlestick Anatomy

The candlestick chart is the most popular way to view price action. Each candlestick represents a specific time period (1 minute, 5 minutes, 1 hour, 1 day, etc.) and shows four pieces of information:

- Open: The price at the start of the period
- High: The highest price reached during the period
- Low: The lowest price reached during the period
- Close: The price at the end of the period

The body is the thick part between open and close:
- Green (or white) body: Close was higher than open. Buyers won that period.
- Red (or black) body: Close was lower than open. Sellers won that period.

The wicks (or shadows) are the thin lines above and below the body:
- Upper wick: Shows the high — buyers pushed up to this level but were pushed back.
- Lower wick: Shows the low — sellers pushed down to this level but buyers pushed back up.

A long upper wick tells you: sellers rejected higher prices. Bearish signal.
A long lower wick tells you: buyers rejected lower prices. Bullish signal.
A large body with tiny wicks tells you: strong conviction in one direction.

Key Candlestick Patterns

Doji: The open and close are nearly the same, creating a tiny body with wicks on both sides. It looks like a cross or plus sign. A doji signals indecision — neither buyers nor sellers won the period. After a strong move up, a doji can signal that momentum is fading. After a strong move down, it can signal that selling pressure is exhausting.

Hammer: A candle with a small body near the top and a long lower wick (at least 2x the body length). It appears after a downtrend and signals potential reversal. The story: sellers pushed the price down hard during the period, but buyers stepped in and pushed it back up near the open. The selling was rejected. A hammer is bullish.

Engulfing Pattern: Two candles where the second candle's body completely engulfs (covers) the first candle's body.
- Bullish engulfing: A small red candle followed by a larger green candle that completely covers it. Shows buyers overwhelmed sellers. Strongest at the bottom of a downtrend or near support.
- Bearish engulfing: A small green candle followed by a larger red candle. Shows sellers overwhelmed buyers. Strongest at the top of an uptrend or near resistance.

Pin Bar: A candle with an extremely long wick on one side and a tiny body on the other. Similar to a hammer but can appear in any context. A pin bar at a key support or resistance level is one of the most reliable price action signals because it shows aggressive rejection of a price level.

Support and Resistance

Support is a price level where buyers consistently step in and prevent the stock from falling further. Think of it as a floor. Every time the stock drops to this level, demand increases and the price bounces.

Resistance is a price level where sellers consistently appear and prevent the stock from rising further. Think of it as a ceiling. Every time the stock rises to this level, supply increases and the price pulls back.

How to identify support and resistance:

1. Look left on the chart. Where did the stock previously bounce or stall? If AAPL bounced off $180 three times in the past two months, $180 is strong support.

2. Round numbers act as psychological levels. $100, $150, $200 — humans place orders at round numbers, creating natural support/resistance.

3. Previous highs become resistance. Previous lows become support. If a stock peaked at $250 and pulled back, $250 is now resistance. To continue higher, it must break through that level.

4. When support breaks, it often becomes resistance (and vice versa). If AAPL breaks below $180 support, that level often acts as resistance if the stock tries to recover. Traders who bought at $180 and are now underwater will sell to break even when it comes back to that price.

The more times a level is tested, the more significant it becomes. A level tested 5 times over 6 months is much stronger than one tested twice in a week.

Trend Structure

An uptrend is defined by higher highs and higher lows. Each peak is higher than the last peak. Each trough is higher than the last trough. The pattern looks like a staircase going up.

Example: AAPL makes a low at $170, bounces to $185 (high), pulls back to $175 (higher low), then rallies to $192 (higher high), pulls back to $182 (higher low), then rallies to $200 (higher high). That is a clear uptrend.

A downtrend is defined by lower highs and lower lows. Each peak is lower than the last. Each trough is lower. A descending staircase.

A trend breaks when the pattern is violated. If a stock in an uptrend makes a lower low, the trend structure is damaged. It might not mean an immediate reversal, but it is a warning sign that the trend is weakening.

Volume Confirmation

Volume tells you the conviction behind a price move. Think of it as the difference between a whisper and a shout.

A price move on high volume is more meaningful than the same move on low volume. If AAPL breaks above $200 on 2x average volume, that breakout has real buying conviction behind it. If it breaks $200 on half the average volume, the breakout is suspect — there might not be enough buyers to sustain the move.

Key volume principles:
- Breakouts should happen on above-average volume. This confirms institutional participation.
- Pullbacks in an uptrend should happen on declining volume. This shows that sellers are not aggressive — they are just taking small profits.
- Volume spikes at lows (capitulation volume) can signal selling exhaustion and a potential bottom.
- Rising price with declining volume is a divergence — the move is losing momentum.

Breakouts vs Fakeouts

A breakout occurs when the price moves above resistance or below support with conviction. A fakeout (or false breakout) occurs when the price briefly crosses a key level but then reverses back.

How to tell the difference:

Volume: A real breakout has a surge in volume. A fakeout often has average or below-average volume.

Close vs wick: If the price pokes above resistance on an intraday wick but closes below it, that is suspicious. A real breakout typically closes above the level — not just touches it.

Retest: Many legitimate breakouts will retest the breakout level. AAPL breaks above $200, pulls back to $200-$201 (the old resistance, now new support), and then continues higher. This retest confirms the breakout. If the retest fails and the price drops back below $200, it was a fakeout.

Context: Breakouts in the direction of the larger trend have higher success rates. AAPL breaking above resistance during a broader uptrend is more likely to hold than AAPL breaking above resistance in the middle of a bear market.

Real Example: TSLA Breakout vs Fakeout

Setup: Tesla (TSLA) has been consolidating between $230 (support) and $250 (resistance) for three weeks.

True breakout scenario:
- TSLA gaps up to $253 on the open with heavy pre-market volume.
- By mid-morning, volume is 1.8x the 30-day average.
- TSLA closes the day at $258, well above the $250 resistance.
- The next day, it dips to $251 (retesting the breakout level) and bounces.
- Over the next week, TSLA runs to $275.
- Signal confirmation: high volume, close above resistance, successful retest.

Fakeout scenario:
- TSLA pushes to $252 in the first hour of trading, briefly clearing $250 resistance.
- Volume is only average — no notable increase.
- By lunchtime, the price fades back to $248, closing below $250.
- The upper wick on the daily candle extends above $250 but the body closes below.
- Traders who bought the "breakout" at $252 are now trapped with a loss.
- Over the next two days, TSLA drops back to $235.
- Signal: low volume, close back below resistance, no follow-through.

The lesson: never chase a breakout blindly. Wait for confirmation — volume, a close above the level, or a successful retest. It is better to enter slightly late and have a real breakout than to jump in early on a fakeout.

Key Takeaways

1. Price action reads the raw market — no lagging indicators needed.
2. Candlestick wicks reveal rejection. Long wick = that price level was rejected.
3. Hammer and engulfing patterns at key levels are high-probability signals.
4. Support is a floor (buyers), resistance is a ceiling (sellers). When one breaks, roles reverse.
5. Uptrend = higher highs and higher lows. Downtrend = lower highs and lower lows.
6. Volume confirms moves. Breakouts without volume are suspect.
7. Wait for confirmation before trading breakouts — fakeouts trap impatient traders.
