VOLATILITY: THE FORCE THAT DRIVES OPTIONS PRICING

What Is Volatility?

Volatility measures how much a stock's price moves over a given period. It does not tell you direction — just magnitude. A stock that moves $5 per day is more volatile than one that moves $0.50 per day, regardless of whether those moves are up or down.

Think of it like the ocean. On calm days, the waves are small — the water rises and falls gently. On stormy days, the waves are huge — dramatic swings in both directions. Volatility measures the size of the waves.

For options traders, volatility is everything. It is the single most important factor in determining how much an option costs. Understanding volatility is the difference between overpaying for options and finding bargains.

Historical Volatility (HV): Looking Backward

Historical volatility measures how much a stock has actually moved over a past period. It is calculated from real price data.

If AAPL has a 30-day historical volatility of 25%, it means AAPL's annualized standard deviation of returns over the past 30 days has been 25%. In plain English: AAPL has been moving about 1.6% per day on average (25% / sqrt(252 trading days) = ~1.6%).

HV is a fact, not an opinion. It tells you what already happened. A stock with 15% HV has been calm. A stock with 60% HV has been a roller coaster.

Uses of HV:
- Comparing current volatility to historical norms. "Is this stock more or less volatile than usual?"
- Establishing a baseline for expected moves.
- Comparing to implied volatility to find mispricing.

Implied Volatility (IV): Looking Forward

Implied volatility is the market's forecast of how much the stock will move in the future. It is not calculated from past prices — it is backed out of current option prices.

Here is the key insight: if options are expensive, it means the market expects big moves. If options are cheap, the market expects calm. IV is the number that, when plugged into an options pricing model, produces the current market price.

If AAPL has an IV of 35%, the market expects AAPL to move about 35% annualized, or roughly 2.2% per day. This is higher than its 25% HV, meaning the market is pricing in MORE movement than AAPL has shown recently. Options are relatively expensive.

The critical relationship: When IV is higher than HV, options are expensive (the market is pricing in more movement than has occurred). When IV is lower than HV, options are cheap (the market is pricing in less movement than has occurred).

IV Rank and IV Percentile

Raw IV numbers are hard to compare. Is 30% IV high for AAPL? Low? You need context.

IV Rank tells you where current IV falls relative to its range over the past year.

Formula: IV Rank = (Current IV - 52-week Low IV) / (52-week High IV - 52-week Low IV)

Example: AAPL's IV range over the past year has been 18% (low) to 45% (high). Current IV is 30%.
IV Rank = (30 - 18) / (45 - 18) = 12 / 27 = 44%

An IV Rank of 44% means current IV is 44% of the way between its yearly low and high. Middle of the road — neither cheap nor expensive.

IV Percentile tells you what percentage of trading days over the past year had IV below the current level.

If IV Percentile is 80%, it means IV was lower than today on 80% of trading days in the past year. Current IV is relatively high.

Rules of thumb:
- IV Rank/Percentile below 25%: IV is low. Options are cheap. Consider buying options.
- IV Rank/Percentile above 75%: IV is high. Options are expensive. Consider selling options.
- Between 25-75%: Neutral territory. Strategy depends on other factors.

The VIX: The Market's Fear Gauge

The VIX (CBOE Volatility Index) measures the implied volatility of S&P 500 options over the next 30 days. It is often called the "fear gauge" because it spikes when investors are scared.

VIX levels and what they mean:
- Below 15: Market is calm, complacent. Investors expect smooth sailing. Often seen during bull markets.
- 15-20: Normal. Moderate uncertainty. Typical market conditions.
- 20-30: Elevated anxiety. Usually accompanies market pullbacks or uncertainty about Fed policy, elections, or geopolitical events.
- 30-40: Fear. Significant market stress. Often seen during corrections (-10% or more).
- Above 40: Panic. Rare and extreme. COVID crash hit VIX 82. Financial crisis hit 80. These are "once in a few years" events.

The VIX tends to spike fast and decline slowly. Fear arrives overnight; calm returns gradually. This creates a pattern: selling options when VIX is elevated and buying them when VIX is low tends to be profitable over time, because VIX usually overstates future volatility (the market is more scared than warranted).

Volatility Crush: The Post-Event Collapse

Volatility crush is one of the most important concepts for options traders. It happens when IV drops sharply after an anticipated event occurs — most commonly earnings announcements, FDA decisions, or Federal Reserve meetings.

The mechanism:
1. Before the event: Nobody knows the outcome. Uncertainty is high. IV rises because the market expects a big move.
2. After the event: The outcome is known. Uncertainty evaporates. IV collapses — often by 30-60% overnight.
3. Effect on options: Even if the stock moves in your favor, the IV collapse destroys option value.

This is why buying options right before earnings is one of the most common beginner mistakes.

When to Buy Options vs When to Sell Options

Buy options (long premium) when:
- IV is LOW relative to history (IV Rank below 25-30%)
- You expect a volatility expansion (an event, catalyst, or regime change is coming)
- You have a directional thesis and want leveraged exposure
- The event is far enough away that IV has not yet been priced in

Sell options (short premium) when:
- IV is HIGH relative to history (IV Rank above 60-75%)
- You expect volatility to contract (post-event, return to normalcy)
- You want to generate income and are comfortable with the risk
- You have a defined range or probability-based thesis

The edge in selling options: implied volatility overestimates actual realized volatility about 85% of the time. This means option prices, on average, are slightly inflated. Sellers collect this "insurance premium" and, more often than not, keep it. But when they lose, the losses can be large — that is the tradeoff.

Event-Driven Volatility

Certain events predictably cause IV to spike:

Earnings announcements: The most common. IV typically starts rising 5-10 days before earnings and collapses the day after. Every stock has an "earnings IV" cycle.

FOMC meetings: Federal Reserve interest rate decisions. The entire market's IV (VIX) rises into FOMC meetings and drops after the announcement, assuming no surprises.

FDA decisions: For biotech and pharmaceutical stocks, FDA approval or rejection of a drug is a binary event. IV can reach 150-200% before an FDA decision. After the announcement, IV collapses regardless of the outcome.

Macro data releases: Jobs reports, CPI (inflation) data, GDP numbers. These move the broad market and cause short-term IV spikes.

Geopolitical events: Wars, elections, trade policy changes. Less predictable, but when they happen, VIX spikes and individual stock IVs rise across the board.

Real Example: NFLX Options Before vs After Earnings

The setup: Netflix (NFLX) is trading at $600. Earnings are in 3 days.

Before earnings:
- IV is at 75% (compared to its normal 30%). IV Rank is 90%.
- The NFLX $600 ATM call expiring in 10 days is priced at $25.00 ($2,500 per contract).
- The market is pricing in a move of about +/- $35 (5.8%) around earnings.

The straddle price (ATM call + ATM put) tells you the expected move. If the straddle costs $50.00, the market expects NFLX to move about $50 in either direction.

After earnings — Scenario A (stock moves as expected):
- NFLX beats earnings and rises to $630 (+$30).
- IV collapses from 75% to 32%.
- Your $600 call now has $30 of intrinsic value but very little extrinsic value.
- New option price: approximately $32.00. Your profit: $32 - $25 = $7.00 ($700 per contract).
- The stock moved $30 in your favor, but the IV crush ate most of the potential gain.

After earnings — Scenario B (stock barely moves):
- NFLX reports in-line results. Stock moves to $605 (+$5).
- IV collapses from 75% to 32%.
- Your $600 call: $5 of intrinsic value, almost no extrinsic.
- New option price: approximately $6.00. Your loss: $6 - $25 = -$19.00 (-$1,900 per contract).
- The stock went UP and you lost 76% of your investment.

After earnings — Scenario C (stock drops):
- NFLX misses subscriber estimates. Stock drops to $565 (-$35).
- IV collapses from 75% to 35%.
- Your $600 call: $0 intrinsic value (OTM), tiny extrinsic.
- New option price: approximately $1.00. Your loss: $1 - $25 = -$24.00 (-$2,400 per contract, nearly a total loss).

The lesson: When buying options into earnings, you are not just betting on direction. You are betting that the stock moves MORE than the market expects. In Scenario A, NFLX moved $30 but the market had already priced in a $35 move. You barely profited despite being right on direction.

A more sophisticated approach: If you are bullish on NFLX earnings, consider a call debit spread instead of a naked call. Buy the $600 call and sell the $620 call. This reduces your IV crush exposure because you are both long and short vega. Or, if you want to capitalize on the IV crush, sell a put credit spread — you collect the inflated premium and profit as IV collapses (as long as the stock does not drop too much).

Key Takeaways

1. Volatility measures magnitude of price movement, not direction.
2. Historical volatility looks backward. Implied volatility looks forward.
3. IV Rank and IV Percentile tell you whether current IV is high or low relative to history.
4. Buy options when IV is low. Sell options when IV is high.
5. The VIX is the market's fear gauge. Spikes fast, declines slowly.
6. IV crush after earnings can destroy option value even when the stock moves in your favor.
7. Before trading options around events, compare the expected move (straddle price) to your directional thesis. If the stock needs to move more than the expected move for you to profit, the odds are against you.
