OPTIONS CHAIN ANALYSIS: READING THE MENU BEFORE YOU ORDER

What Is an Options Chain?

An options chain is the complete list of all available option contracts for a given stock, organized by expiration date and strike price. Think of it as a restaurant menu: it shows you everything available, with prices, and helps you pick the right item for your appetite and budget.

Every brokerage displays the options chain when you look up options for a stock. On the left side, you see calls. On the right side, puts. Down the middle, you see strike prices. Each row is a different strike, and each column gives you data about that specific contract.

Understanding how to read an options chain is essential before placing any options trade. It tells you what things cost, how liquid they are, and what the market expects.

Columns Explained

Here is what each column in an options chain means, using a simplified AAPL chain with the stock at $190:

Strike: The price at which you can buy (call) or sell (put) the stock. Example: $180, $185, $190, $195, $200. Strikes above the current price are OTM for calls, ITM for puts. Strikes below are ITM for calls, OTM for puts.

Bid: The highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay for the option. If you are selling an option, the bid is approximately what you will receive. Example: AAPL $190 call bid = $4.80.

Ask: The lowest price a seller is currently willing to accept. If you are buying an option, the ask is approximately what you will pay. Example: AAPL $190 call ask = $5.00.

Last: The price of the most recent trade. This can be misleading if the last trade happened hours ago and the stock has moved since. Always look at bid and ask for the current market.

Volume: The number of contracts traded today at this strike. High volume means lots of activity. Example: if the $190 call shows volume of 12,500, that is 12,500 contracts (1.25 million shares worth) traded today. Significant activity.

Open Interest (OI): The total number of contracts that currently exist and are open (not yet closed or exercised). Unlike volume which resets to zero each day, open interest accumulates. Example: the $190 call has OI of 45,000 — meaning 45,000 contracts are currently held by traders.

Implied Volatility (IV): The market's expectation of how much the stock will move before expiration, expressed as an annualized percentage. Higher IV = more expensive options. Example: IV of 30% means the market expects AAPL to move roughly 30% annualized, or about 1.7% per week.

Open Interest vs Volume: What Each Tells You

Volume tells you what happened today. Open interest tells you what has accumulated.

High volume with low open interest: New interest at this strike today. Traders are opening new positions. This is fresh activity.

Low volume with high open interest: Existing positions sit at this strike, but nobody is trading them today. These are older positions.

High volume AND high open interest: An active, liquid strike with lots of existing positions and lots of trading today. This is where you want to trade — execution will be clean.

Volume exceeding open interest is a special signal: it means more contracts traded today than existed at the start of the day. This indicates significant new positioning, which can signal smart money activity.

Bid-Ask Spread: Why It Matters

The bid-ask spread is the cost of doing business. It is the gap between what buyers offer and sellers ask.

Tight spread example: Bid $4.80, Ask $5.00. Spread = $0.20 ($20 per contract).
Wide spread example: Bid $2.10, Ask $2.80. Spread = $0.70 ($70 per contract).

Why the spread matters: If you buy an option at the ask and immediately try to sell it, you would sell at the bid — losing the spread. A $0.70 spread means you start the trade $70 in the hole per contract. That is a headwind you must overcome just to break even.

Rules for spreads:
- Liquid options (AAPL, SPY, QQQ, MSFT) have tight spreads: $0.01-$0.10. Good for trading.
- Less liquid stocks might have $0.30-$1.00 spreads. These eat into your profits significantly.
- Always use limit orders, not market orders, for options. Place your order between the bid and ask (the "mid" price) and see if you get filled. For example, with a $4.80/$5.00 spread, try offering $4.90.

Implied Volatility Across Strikes: The Volatility Smile/Skew

If you look at the IV for each strike price, you will notice something interesting: IV is not the same everywhere. Typically, OTM puts have higher IV than ATM options, and far OTM calls also have slightly higher IV.

This pattern is called the volatility skew (or smile).

Why? Because the market prices in "crash risk." OTM puts protect against big downside moves, and demand for that protection drives up their IV. The 2008 financial crisis, COVID crash, and other violent selloffs have taught the market that tail risks are real. OTM puts are in higher demand than OTM calls, so they cost more (higher IV).

The practical takeaway: OTM puts are relatively expensive. If you are buying them for protection, you are paying a premium. If you are selling them, you are being compensated for taking on crash risk.

Unusual Options Activity

Sometimes you will see a massive spike in volume at a specific strike — 10x or 50x the normal activity. This is called unusual options activity (UOA).

What it might signal:
- A large institution placing a directional bet
- Someone with inside knowledge (legally or illegally)
- A hedge fund hedging a large stock position
- A bank rolling a structured product

How to evaluate UOA:
1. Is the volume at a new strike, or is someone closing an existing position? Check OI the next day — if OI increased, new positions were opened.
2. Were the options bought or sold? Check if the trades happened at the ask (bought, bullish for calls) or at the bid (sold, bearish for calls). Some platforms show this.
3. What is the size relative to open interest? If OI is 500 and 5,000 contracts traded, that is dramatic.
4. What is the expiration? Near-term unusual activity (less than 2 weeks to expiry) might signal a catalyst like earnings or an FDA decision.

Do not blindly follow unusual activity. But it is a data point worth noting, especially when it aligns with your own analysis.

Liquidity Assessment: How to Pick Tradeable Strikes

Not all strikes are created equal. Before choosing a strike, assess its liquidity:

Good liquidity indicators:
- Bid-ask spread is $0.10 or less (for stocks under $200)
- Volume is at least 100 contracts today
- Open interest is at least 500 contracts
- The option moves when the stock moves (responsive pricing)

Bad liquidity indicators:
- Bid-ask spread is $0.50 or more
- Volume is 0-10 contracts
- Open interest is under 100
- Stale pricing (the bid/ask has not changed even though the stock moved)

Practical rule: Stick to ATM and near-the-money strikes. The further OTM you go, the less liquid the options become. Stick to expiration cycles with high overall volume — usually the nearest monthly expiration and the weeklies for highly liquid stocks.

Real Example: Walking Through an AAPL Options Chain

Let us look at a simplified AAPL options chain with the stock at $190, for options expiring in 30 days.

CALLS:
Strike | Bid   | Ask   | Volume | OI     | IV    | Delta
$180   | $11.20| $11.40| 1,200  | 15,000 | 28%   | 0.82
$185   | $7.50 | $7.70 | 3,500  | 28,000 | 27%   | 0.68
$190   | $4.80 | $5.00 | 12,500 | 45,000 | 26%   | 0.50
$195   | $2.60 | $2.75 | 8,200  | 35,000 | 27%   | 0.33
$200   | $1.10 | $1.20 | 5,800  | 22,000 | 29%   | 0.18
$205   | $0.40 | $0.48 | 2,100  | 8,000  | 32%   | 0.08

Analysis:

Best liquidity: The $190 strike. Tightest spread ($0.20), highest volume (12,500), highest OI (45,000). This is the ATM strike and the market's focal point.

The $185 and $195 strikes are also very liquid — suitable for trading.

The $205 strike is getting thin: only 2,100 volume, wider relative spread ($0.08 on a $0.44 option is 18%), and the IV is higher (32% vs 26% at ATM), reflecting the skew for OTM options.

If you are moderately bullish and want a balance of cost and probability, the $195 call at $2.75 (delta 0.33, about 33% chance of expiring ITM) is a reasonable choice. Your cost is $275 per contract, and you need AAPL above $197.75 at expiration to profit.

If you want a higher probability trade, the $185 call at $7.70 (delta 0.68) gives you more exposure to the stock move but costs $770 per contract.

Notice how the chain gives you everything you need to make an informed decision: the cost, the probability (via delta), the liquidity (via spread, volume, OI), and the market's volatility expectation (via IV).

Key Takeaways

1. The options chain is your map — learn to read every column before trading.
2. Bid-ask spread is a real cost. Use limit orders and trade liquid strikes.
3. Volume tells you today's activity. Open interest tells you the accumulated position.
4. IV varies across strikes (skew). OTM puts are typically more expensive.
5. Unusual options activity can signal institutional positioning, but do not trade it blindly.
6. Stick to strikes with tight spreads, high volume, and high open interest.
7. The ATM strike almost always has the best liquidity.
